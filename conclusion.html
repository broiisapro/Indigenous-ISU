Conclusion: Synthesizing Our Discoveries

Having explored various facets of Bad Cree through our podcast, novel presentation, and research, this concluding section brings together our key insights and final reflections.

Summary of Key Findings

The Bad Cree by Jessica Johns is a complex text that employs supernatural horror to focus on intergenerational trauma, Indigenous resistance, and spiritual renewal.

The horrors lie in colonial histories—economic decline, environmental destruction from resource extraction, and “man camps”—and how those losses manifest as grief and trauma for characters like Mackenzie.

The wîhtikô embodies the monstrous consequences of uncontrolled greed, settler colonialism, and unresolved trauma. It links visible destruction (the land) with invisible suffering (spiritual decay), showing that trauma cannot be neatly compartmentalized.

Mackenzie’s interior struggles—disassociation, broken memory, self-doubt—are manifestations of psychological trauma inherited from residential schools and the Indian Act, which influence her coping strategies and feelings of internalized shame.

Issues like addiction (Tracy) are framed not as moral failings, but as public health issues rooted in systemic inequality.

The text elevates Indigenous women and their knowledge as key sources of resilience—through matriarchs, ancestral dreams, and ceremonies.

Dreams are not merely subconscious but are Cree ways of communicating with other realms, emphasizing Cree ontology.

The novel aligns with works like Telling Our Twisted Histories and Dead White Writer on the Floor, challenging colonial narratives and stereotypes (e.g., Disney’s Pocahontas).

It echoes the Idle No More movement’s values: reclaiming lives from capitalist forces, restoring environmental justice, and affirming Indigenous sovereignty.

The Indian Act, TRC, and UNDRIP are key frameworks presented in the novel that represent both the trauma and the paths toward healing.

Broader Significance and Lasting Impact

The Bad Cree is an important work in Indigenous literature, not only deconstructing colonial trauma but also reclaiming stolen narratives and reconnecting spiritual ties.

It illustrates how traditional Indigenous knowledge and beliefs provide powerful pathways to address intergenerational trauma and ecological crises.

The novel subverts Western ideas of healing by centering place-based, culturally relevant care and honoring Indigenous practices.

It decolonizes the horror genre by presenting culturally grounded, complex characters who resist colonial tropes and assert identity.

This work reinforces the ongoing relevance of resistance movements like Idle No More and emphasizes environmental justice by tying spiritual identity to land.

It reminds readers of the urgency behind TRC’s Calls to Action and UNDRIP as blueprints for real reconciliation.

Personal Reflections and Future Inquiry

Reading The Bad Cree has reshaped my understanding of historical trauma, environmental injustice, and Indigenous spirituality. The metaphor of the wîhtikô as colonial greed made me realize how systemic oppression becomes normalized.

I was especially moved by the matriarchal strength and traditional healing practices embedded in the story. The wisdom and resilience of Indigenous peoples continues to inspire me.

In the future, I want to explore how Indigenous authors use genre fiction to engage with political issues, as well as the real-world applications of TRC and UNDRIP in shaping policy and healing.

This novel has encouraged me to continue amplifying Indigenous voices and contribute to a more informed, just understanding of Indigenous cultures, histories, and futures.
